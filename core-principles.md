# Core Principles: Marketing for Developer Audiences

## Principle 1: Earn trust before asking for attention

Developers don't owe you their attention. They've built mental filters that block most marketing automatically — because most marketing that reaches them is irrelevant, inaccurate, or condescending.

Trust is earned through:
- **Accuracy.** Get the technical details right. One wrong claim in a blog post and you lose a developer permanently.
- **Specificity.** "Better performance" means nothing. "Renames a function across 200 files in under a second" means something.
- **Usefulness.** Give before you ask. A tutorial that actually helps them solve a problem is worth 20 product announcements.

**What this means in practice:**
- Technical content should be reviewed by a developer before publishing. Not for "clarity" — for accuracy.
- Claims need to be demonstrable. If you can't show it, don't say it.
- Educational content that doesn't mention your product is legitimate and valuable.

---

## Principle 2: Show, don't tell. Then don't tell at all.

Developer audiences respond to demonstration. Not to descriptions of demonstration. Not to testimonials about how good the demonstration was. The actual demonstration.

This is why product demos, code examples, and "try it yourself" flows outperform almost every other developer marketing format.

**The corollary:** Written marketing copy for developers should be the minimum needed to get them to the demonstration. The copy's job is to answer "why should I spend 3 minutes watching this?" — not to replace the demonstration.

**What this means in practice:**
- Every campaign should have a demo artifact at its center: a video, a sandbox, an interactive example, a real code sample.
- Landing pages should lead to "try it" faster than they lead to "learn more."
- Case studies should include what the developer actually did, not just what the marketing team wishes they'd said.

---

## Principle 3: Respect their time and intelligence simultaneously

Developers are fast readers who are also busy. They scan first, then read if the scan passes.

The scan needs to answer:
1. Is this for someone like me?
2. Is this about something I care about?
3. Will this be accurate and useful, or marketing noise?

If the scan fails, they're gone. If the scan passes but the content is dumbed down or padded with filler, they're also gone.

**The trap:** Writing to seem accessible when your audience is already expert. A senior TypeScript developer doesn't need you to explain what TypeScript is.

**What this means in practice:**
- Write for the experienced reader. Link out or footnote for context — don't pad the main content.
- Headlines should be informative, not clever. "WebStorm 2025.3: what's new for TypeScript developers" > "WebStorm just got a major upgrade"
- Cut every sentence that doesn't add information.

## Principle 4: Communities are not channels

The instinct to treat developer communities (forums, Slack groups, Discord servers, open source projects) as distribution channels is understandable and wrong.

Developers in communities are there for the community — for peer connection, problem solving, and shared craft. The moment marketing enters those spaces with broadcast intent, it reads as violation.

**What works instead:**
- Participate genuinely before you promote anything
- Contribute value the community would want even if your product didn't exist
- Create spaces — don't colonize existing ones
- Let community members choose to spread your message; don't ask them to

**The urban sketching parallel:** I run an urban sketching community in Cyprus. The community grew 10x in year one — not from promotion, but from organizing genuine shared experiences: drawing together in public, exhibitions, workshops. People promoted it to other people because they valued it. That's the only sustainable model. Developer communities work identically.

## Principle 5: The anti-hype position is a position

When every competitor is claiming "revolutionary AI-powered next-generation" something, restraint becomes a differentiator.

Developers are deeply attuned to hype language. They use it as a signal of unreliability. A marketing voice that says "here's what actually changed, here's specifically how it helps you, here's what it doesn't do yet" is rare and therefore trusted.

**What this means in practice:**
- Don't oversell releases. A minor quality-of-life improvement is worth communicating honestly as exactly that.
- Acknowledge limitations. "This works best for X and less well for Y" builds more trust than pretending Y doesn't exist.
- If your product genuinely does something exceptional — be specific, be direct, and let the specificity do the selling.

---

## Principle 6: The developer who becomes your advocate is worth 1,000 impressions

Word-of-mouth drives developer tool adoption more than almost any paid channel. A developer who tweets "I switched to WebStorm and the TypeScript refactoring is genuinely better — here's why" reaches their 3,000 followers with a message that has their credibility behind it.

That's worth a budget line.

**What this means in practice:**
- Identify developers who genuinely love your product. Make them feel known and valued — not as "brand ambassadors" (they'll find that language off-putting) but as contributors to something they care about.
- Give power users early access, direct lines to the team, acknowledgment in release notes.
- Make it easy to share specific moments of delight: "click to tweet" on key demo moments, shareable code snippets, changelog entries written to be quotable.
